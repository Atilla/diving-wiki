# The Hammerhead Triangle — Eastern Tropical Pacific Shark Corridor

The three great hammerhead aggregation sites of the Eastern Tropical Pacific — **Galapagos (Darwin/Wolf)**, **Cocos Island**, and **Malpelo** — are not isolated populations. They are connected by the **Cocos Ridge**, a 700 km submarine mountain chain that acts as a migratory highway for scalloped hammerheads, whale sharks, sea turtles, and other pelagics.

- **Satellite tracking has confirmed** individual hammerheads tagged at Malpelo traveling to Cocos and Galapagos, and vice versa. In 2025, researchers documented for the first time a scalloped hammerhead migrating from Panama to Galapagos via the corridor.
- **CMAR (Eastern Tropical Pacific Marine Corridor):** a joint initiative by Ecuador, Costa Rica, Colombia, and Panama connecting five MPAs — Galapagos, Cocos Island, Coiba (Panama), Gorgona, and Malpelo. The Hermandad Marine Reserve (Ecuador, 2022, ~60,000 km2) extended Galapagos protections northeast along the Cocos Ridge, and Costa Rica expanded its Cocos protections to create a functional corridor.
- **$15.6 million GEF funding** approved for "Beyond 30x30" resilience initiative across the CMAR. A 10-year Action Plan (2025–2035) guides conservation, enforcement, and monitoring. A marine law enforcement strengthening project launched in 2024 with international funding.
- **UNESCO Transboundary Marine Biosphere Reserve** designation further links all four nations' conservation efforts.
- **The corridor's importance for divers:** these three destinations are the world's premier hammerhead diving. Doing all three in separate trips is the ultimate shark diving trilogy. The corridor also means that conservation at one site benefits the others — and threats (longline fishing outside MPA boundaries) affect all three. The 73.3% hammerhead decline documented at Malpelo between 2009–2019 and the 2026–2035 coral projections at Darwin/Wolf are warning signs that the window for peak encounters may be narrowing.
