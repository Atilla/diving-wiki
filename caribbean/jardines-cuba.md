# Jardines de la Reina

## Status

**SEVERELY DEGRADED reef + EFFECTIVELY INACCESSIBLE due to geopolitical crisis**

**Geopolitical situation (April 2026):** US-Cuba tensions have escalated sharply under the current administration, with intensified sanctions, severed diplomatic channels, and credible discussion of military intervention. This has made travel to Cuba extremely difficult and risky for trip planning purposes:

- **US citizens**: OFAC restrictions have been further tightened beyond the already-complex 2025 sanctions. Legal pathways for American divers are essentially closed for the foreseeable future.
- **Non-US citizens**: European and Canadian divers can technically still travel, but Cuba's collapsing tourism infrastructure (airlines cutting routes, hotels closing, fuel shortages affecting boat operations) makes logistics unreliable. Avalon's operations are reportedly running at reduced capacity.
- **Insurance and safety**: Travel insurers are restricting or excluding Cuba coverage. Medical evacuation options have narrowed. The recompression situation (already limited) is further compromised.
- **Recommendation: Do not plan trips to Jardines de la Reina for 2026–2027.** The combination of geopolitical instability, sanctions risk, infrastructure collapse, and degraded reef condition makes this destination nonviable for responsible trip planning. Monitor the situation — if political conditions stabilize, the shark diving may still justify a visit for the right diver, but that window is not now.

**Reef condition (for the record):**

- Decline predates 2023 bleaching: 2019 surveys showed 24-41% algal cover. 17-year reef crest studies show macroalgae expanding to 77-87% at some sites by 2021.
- Summer 2023 bleaching was catastrophic: 34C at 27m depth. ScubaBoard Jan 2024: healthy coral cover estimated **under 5%**, "only color came from sponges."
- A repeat visitor (4 trips, 3 pre-COVID): *"very disappointing."* Feb 2025 reviewer: *"calling these the Caribbean's most pristine reefs today is misleading."*
- Enforcement severely strained: ~300+ illegal fishing boats daily (confirmed by Global Conservation), few government patrol boats, no radar. Avalon operates a dedicated protection vessel patrolling ~4,000 miles/year, 300+ days/year — but this single private effort cannot substitute for systematic state enforcement.
- COVID/economic crisis gutted funding: Cuba tourism down 48% vs 2019, ~200% hyperinflation. Coral recruitment failing — "baby corals are not coming back."
- **What remains (when accessible)**: Sharks (5-15 per dive), exceptional fish biomass, crocodiles, sponges. Go for sharks only, not coral.

## Key Dive Sites

#### Pipin

Wall dive (drop-off at 12m, descending past 40m). Named after freediver Pipin Ferreras. Black coral, large barrel sponges. Caribbean reef sharks patrol the wall edge reliably.

#### Farallon

Coral heads and channels (10–25m). Silky sharks in open water off the reef edge. Large cubera and dog snapper aggregations.

#### La Cueva

Swim-through and cavern system at 15–22m. Nurse sharks resting in overhangs. Massive Nassau and black groupers.

#### Cabezo de las Carolinas

Spur-and-groove reef (8–20m). Dense fish biomass: schooling jacks, tarpon stacked in channels, permit. One of the most "fish-saturated" dives in the Caribbean.

Structurally, classic Caribbean reef architecture — spur-and-groove, wall drop-offs, patch reefs separated by sand channels — but with biomass density recalling pre-exploitation Caribbean ecosystems.

## Marine Life

- **Sharks**: Caribbean reef sharks near-guaranteed — often 6–15 per dive at baited and unbaited sites. Silky sharks at outer walls and safety stops. Nurse sharks under ledges. Encounter rates remain among the Caribbean's highest, though some operators report modest declines from 2010s peak.
- **Crocodile encounters**: American crocodiles (*Crocodylus acutus*) inhabit mangrove channels between dive sites. Guided snorkeling sessions in shallow (<2m) mangrove creeks. Not guaranteed but frequent in warmer months.
- **Grouper and tarpon**: Nassau and black grouper conspicuously large and unafraid — behavior virtually extinct elsewhere in the Caribbean. Atlantic tarpon school in channels (groups of 20–50). Cubera snapper regularly exceed 15kg.
- **Overall biomass**: Historically estimated at 5–10x higher than comparable Caribbean reefs, though recent reports suggest erosion of this advantage as enforcement has wavered.

## Dive Conditions

- **Visibility**: 20–40m, typically 25–30m.
- **Temperature**: 26–29C summer, 24–26C winter. 3mm year-round; 5mm for winter comfort.
- **Currents**: Generally mild to moderate. Stronger on outer walls and channels.

## Season

- **Year-round**, but **Nov–Apr** peak (dry season, calmer seas, best visibility). Hurricane risk Aug–Oct.

## Practical Info

- **Access**: Fly to Havana, then domestic connection or ~8hr road transfer to Jucaro (Ciego de Avila province). From Jucaro marina, ~3 hours by boat to the archipelago.
- **Sole operator**: **Avalon Diving Center** holds the exclusive concession. All trips book through Avalon or authorized agents.
- **Accommodation**: Floating hotels (converted houseboats) moored in mangroves — not traditional liveaboards. La Tortuga and similar vessels. Basic but functional; diving by skiff from the mothership.
- **Costs**: ~USD $3,500–5,000 per person for 7-night packages including Havana transfers, diving, meals, permits.
- **US travelers**: Effectively blocked. OFAC restrictions have been further tightened under 2025–2026 sanctions. Legal pathways for recreational diving are essentially nonexistent.
- **Non-US travelers**: European/Canadian citizens can technically travel, but Cuba's collapsing infrastructure (airline route cuts, fuel shortages, hotel closures) makes logistics unreliable. Verify Avalon's operational status before committing funds.
- **Insurance**: Travel insurers increasingly excluding or restricting Cuba coverage. Verify explicitly before booking.
- **Overall**: **Do not book for 2026–2027 without accepting significant risk of cancellation, stranding, or loss of funds.** The geopolitical situation may improve, but planning around that assumption is inadvisable.
